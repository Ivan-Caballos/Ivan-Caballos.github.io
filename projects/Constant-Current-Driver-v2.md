# Laser Diode Constant-Current Driver — V2 (MOSFET)

**Status:** Simulation verified and breadboard validated  
**Focus:** Closed-loop current regulation, MOSFET linear-region behaviour, and practical bench verification

---

## Project Objective

This project developed and validated a MOSFET-based second iteration of the constant-current driver, intended as a more robust follow-on to the initial BJT version.

The main goal was to confirm that the same closed-loop control law remained valid with a logic-level N-MOSFET as the pass element, while also capturing the practical gate-drive, headroom, and dissipation conditions required for linear operation up to approximately **200 mA**.

---

## Why this version matters

V2 was not a completely different concept. It was an architectural refinement of the original driver.

The project helped verify:

- that the feedback loop still regulated correctly with a MOSFET pass element
- that output current followed the expected relationship **I ≈ VSET / RSENSE**
- that the op-amp could provide the required **VGS** to support linear operation across the tested range
- that measured hardware behaviour remained consistent with simulation once the circuit was functioning correctly

This made V2 a more practical platform for continued current-driver development.

---

## Operating Principle

This is a **closed-loop current regulation** circuit.

- A reference voltage **VSET** is generated by a divider/potentiometer and applied to the op-amp **V+**
- The op-amp compares this against the sensed voltage across **RSENSE**, connected to **V−**
- The op-amp drives the MOSFET gate so that the sensed voltage matches the setpoint

When the loop is in regulation, **V+ ≈ V−**, so the shunt voltage across **RSENSE** follows **VSET**.

As a result:

**I_LOAD ≈ VSET / RSENSE**

In this version, the pass element is an **IRLZ44N logic-level N-MOSFET** operating in the **linear region**, effectively acting as a controlled variable resistance.

## Simulation Setup

- **Supply:** 5 V DC
- **Op-amp:** LM358
- **Pass device:** IRLZ44N (N-MOSFET)
- **Sense resistor:** RSENSE = 4.7 Ω
- **Load (simple laser emulator):** 2 × 1N4001 in series + RLOAD = 4.7 Ω
- **Reference network:** RTOP = 39.4 kΩ, RV1 = 10 kΩ potentiometer
- **Gate network:** RGATE = 100 Ω, RPD = 100 kΩ
- **Feedback resistor present:** RFB = 330 Ω

Primary validation method:
- confirm **V+ ≈ V−**
- verify current through **RSENSE**
- inspect **gate, source, and drain voltages** to understand MOSFET operating conditions

---

## Full-Scale Simulation Verification

At full-scale adjustment:

- **VSET / Op-amp V+ ≈ 1.02 V**
- **Op-amp V− ≈ 1.02 V**

This confirms regulation:

**V+ ≈ V−**

Current from the shunt:

**I ≈ VRSENSE / RSENSE = 1.02 V / 4.7 Ω ≈ 0.217 A (217 mA)**

Measured gate/source operating point:

- **Gate voltage ≈ 3.19 V**
- **Source / sense node ≈ 1.02 V**

Therefore:

`VGS ≈ 3.19 V − 1.02 V ≈ 2.17 V`

Additional node above the MOSFET:

- **Drain-side node ≈ 2.33 V**

Approximate device headroom:

`VDS ≈ 2.33 V − 1.02 V ≈ 1.31 V`

### Interpretation

The simulation confirmed that the MOSFET version preserved the intended control behaviour while requiring a meaningful positive **VGS** to sustain current in the linear region. It also showed that the device retained positive **VDS** headroom at full scale, allowing regulation to remain valid.

## Breadboard Verification

Bench measurements were taken using the same core topology and the same current-verification method:

**I ≈ VRSENSE / RSENSE**

Measured supply at the breadboard:

- **VSUPPLY ≈ 4.97 V**

During bring-up, a faulty or out-of-spec **LM358** initially prevented the loop from closing properly, with **V−** remaining below **V+** despite apparently sufficient device headroom. Replacing the op-amp restored normal regulation and brought measured behaviour back into line with simulation.

This was a useful reminder that practical validation includes component sanity-checking, not just circuit theory.

---

## Full-Scale Breadboard Measurement

Measured node voltages at approximately full-scale current:

- **V+ = 0.98 V**
- **V− = 0.97 V**
- **Op-amp output = 3.21 V**
- **MOSFET gate = 3.19 V**
- **MOSFET drain = 2.41 V**
- **MOSFET source = 0.97 V**

Current from the shunt:

**I ≈ 0.97 V / 4.7 Ω ≈ 0.206 A (≈ 206 mA)**

Derived operating point:

- `VGS ≈ 3.19 − 0.97 ≈ 2.22 V`
- `VDS ≈ 2.41 − 0.97 ≈ 1.44 V`

Estimated dissipation:

- `MOSFET dissipation ≈ I · VDS ≈ 0.206 · 1.44 ≈ 0.30 W`
- `RSENSE dissipation ≈ I²R ≈ (0.206²) · 4.7 ≈ 0.20 W`

### Interpretation

The measured data confirmed that the loop was regulating correctly in hardware and that the MOSFET required roughly **2.1–2.2 V of VGS** to support this operating range in linear mode.

Thermally, the MOSFET became only slightly warm during short tests, consistent with the estimated dissipation.

### Interpretation

Several useful patterns appeared across the measured range:

- **The loop remained in regulation**, with **V+ ≈ V−** typically within about **10 mV**
- **Measured current stayed slightly above nominal** at lower currents, which is consistent with resistor tolerance, op-amp offset, and potentiometer resolution effects at small **VSET**
- **VDS remained positive throughout the tested range**, confirming adequate headroom for regulation
- **Gate voltage rose to around 2.3–2.9 V**, showing the non-trivial drive conditions needed to maintain MOSFET operation in the linear region

Overall, the measured behaviour was consistent with the simulation model and confirmed the practical viability of the MOSFET architecture.

---

## Engineering Takeaways

- **The same control law still applies:** when the loop is in regulation, **I ≈ VSET / RSENSE**
- **A MOSFET used in linear mode requires meaningful gate drive**, even at relatively modest current
- **Checking VGS and VDS is essential** when validating a MOSFET current sink in real hardware
- **Bring-up requires component-level scepticism:** replacing the faulty LM358 restored normal loop behaviour and prevented a misleading diagnosis
- **At these current levels, MOSFET dissipation remained manageable**, making this version a stronger platform for continued development than the original BJT implementation
